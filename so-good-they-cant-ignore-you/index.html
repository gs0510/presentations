The myth of meritocracy

The intent to be meritocratic is not a myth, but we know what road is paved with good intentions. … It’s time to apply our heartfelt belief that as engineers we are rational, and rationally accept what the data is telling us: it isn’t a meritocracy. We are all biased.

What is a meritocracy?

The belief that everyone has an equal opportunity for success based on personal achivement and merit.

tech certainly intends to be a meritocracy. We are supposed to reward merit and impact, not politics or who-you-know. I’m not saying nepotism doesn’t happen, but it is against the rules in a way it might not be in other industries. It’s particularly hard for powerful people like CEOs to see tech as anything but a meritocracy because the system rewarded them, and so that must have been on merit, right? You will find that female CEOs hold exactly the same beliefs, though we’d never be so crass as to call them sexist for it.

The Problem with Meritocracy

Individuals can and should be measured on the basis of their intellectual contributions, divorced from identity, gender, race, or other distinguishing characteristics.

The first problem with this is the assumption of equal access and information that meritocracy relies on is clearly missing in the tech community. Even thought we have the internet and access to vast information, it can't be said that we have equal access to information. “We are humans, working with humans, to develop software for the benefit of humans.” A significant problem with our already flawed notion of meritocracy: within this system, the worth of the individual is measured not by their humanity, but solely by their intellectual output.

Meritocracy is based on a utilitarian interpretation of our work.

Utilitarianism as a philosophical system states that the most moral action is the one that maximizes utility: in essence, that the end justifies the means. But one of the well-noted problems of adopting utility as the primary measure of morality is that it ignores the concept of justice, as cruel actions are condoned so long as they contribute to the greater good. In its rejection of social justice, utilitarianism is essentially a dehumanizing philosophy. It objectifies people, reducing them to the level of tools or instruments for the “greater good”, which in most cases is code for whatever delivers maximum comfort.

The paradox of meritocracy

Emilio J. Castilla, a professor at MIT’s Sloan School of Management, has explored how meritocratic ideals and HR practices like pay-for-performance play out in organizations, and he’s come to some unexpected conclusions. But Castilla’s analysis revealed some very non-meritocratic outcomes. Women, ethnic minorities, and non-U.S.-born employees received a smaller increase in compensation compared with white men, despite holding the same jobs, working in the same units, having the same supervisors, the same human capital, and importantly, receiving the same performance score. The paradox of meritocracy builds on other research showing that those who think they are the most objective can actually exhibit the most bias in their evaluations. To summarize, no one cares about your work as much as you do.

How to make your work visible!

Two of our biggest challenges include developing our personal brand and proving our value. At some point in time in your career, you will have to prove your value, but it can be hard to talk about your achievements without underselling yourself, or feeling like you’re boasting. Hopefully we can help you overcome this hurdle by providing you with some proven tools to help you hype yourself! There’s this idea that, if you do great work at your job, people will (or should!) automatically recognize that work and reward you for it with promotions / increased pay. In practice, it’s often more complicated than that – some kinds of important work are more visible/memorable than others. It’s frustrating to have done something really important and later realize that you didn’t get rewarded for it just because the people making the decision didn’t understand or remember what you did.

Build your personal brand!

Your personal brand can be best described as what people say about you when you are not in the room. It’s a reflection how you are perceived by the world around you. When it comes to your personal brand, its not a question of whether you have one. But whether you conscious choose to guide and cultivate or let it be defined for you.

Personal brand is a platform for growth!

  • Clarity
  • Confidence
  • Authenticity
  • Differentiation
To build your brand you need to: 1. Be clear about what you are trying to achieve 2. Confident in yourself and your ability to achieve your goals 3. Authentic in your actions, and how you choose to pursue your goals 4. Use your unique traits to differentiate you from the crowd

Build an experience map for yourself!

An experience map is a great way to help visualize your brand so that you can tell your brand story. Make it fun! – your experience map should be a reflection of who you are & what makes you unique. Experience maps are a great tool to help you quickly identify your brand when working with new people – whether it’s a boss, mentor, or coworker!

Write a brag doc!

  • You don't remember all the cool things you did!
  • Your manager doesn't remember all the cool things you did!
  • Write a document listing everything you're proud of!
Julia Evans has a great blog post about writing a brag document. So what is a brag document? In most places, every 6 months, or a year or a quarter you have to go through performance reviews, when you're scrambling to think of all the things you did in that year/6 months/quarter. It is not meant to be a resume, a self evaluation or a vision board. Each of those serve their own purpose, but are somewhat different. Instead, it is a living list of your achievements and your accomplishments. You use it to track the small and big impacts that you’ve made. You may have the best manager in the world, but in reality your manager doesn’t know about all the work that you’ve done, and especially what work you’re proudest of. Having a Hype Doc gives your manager a quick and easy list to use for reference. It also makes your life easier if you ever have to write a self evaluation. In the context of creating an Experience Map, it works as an excellent backend database where you can keep track of your achievements, and then once a year you can look through they Hype Doc and use it to update your Experience Map.

What can you add to your brag doc?

  • That Pull Request which took forver to merge :)
  • Design documents you contributed to.
  • A talk you gave!
It doesn't matter if your PR has 3 lines of code of 300 lines of code, if you're proud of it, add it to your brag doc!

Brag doc template!

  • Goals for the year.
  • You code & non-code contributions to a project.
  • Collaboration & mentoring.
  • Helping the company.
  • What you learned?
  • Participation outside of work.
List your major goals here! Sharing your goals with your manager & coworkers is really nice because it helps them see how they can support you in accomplishing those goals! Did you help with the desgin doc, did you help get several teams get onboard with the idea?, what was the impact your work have? Is it numerical, did it help bring in x% more revenue, or did it help pass a non numerical goal like this was required for GDPR. Believing that you're a junior engineer and cannot mentor someone is wrong!! You can totally mentor others, when someone new joins the team, help them with onboarding, mentor an intern, helping other teams trying to use your team's software, giving an internal workshop. Helping out with interviewing, ensuring that the interview process is fair. Participation outside of work could be contributing to a an open source project you like, a blog post, etc.

Don't forget the invisible work!

A lot of us work on fuzzy projects that can feel hard to quantify, like: improving code quality on the team / making code reviews a little more in depth making on call easier building a more fair interview process / performance review system refactoring / driving down technical debt A lot of people will leave this kind of work out because they don’t know how to explain why it’s important. But I think this kind of work is especially important to put into your brag document because it’s the most likely to fall under the radar! Explain the goal, list the things you did to achieve that goal and look at how visible the effects are! Maybe your co-workers can help you figure out how to make the impact more visible.

How to help others?

  • Run a brag document workshop!
  • Setup time biweekly or once a month to update brag docs!
  • Encourage each other!
In general, especially if you’re someone who really cares about your work, I think it’s really positive to share your goals & accomplishments (and the things that haven’t gone so well too!) with your friends and coworkers. It makes it feel less like you’re working alone and more like everyone is supporting each other in helping them accomplish what they want.

What's so great about having a brag doc?

One of the greatest things about having a brag doc in my opinion is you find out immediately when you're not progressing or learning enough. The first time I ever had a review was just after 4 months I started my job, and when I was scrambling to find what I did for the past 4 months, I realized I did not learn as much as I wanted, the work I was doing was not reflecting what my goals were and there were so many things I cared about, for which I did nothing at all. Having a brag doc is a great to immediately find out when things are going not so well and act on it!

Network!

Just like you wouldn’t want someone else to determine your personal brand, you also shouldn’t want others to tell your story. Once you have defined your personal brand – live it with your actions and words. Share it by being your authentic self so that others around you start to see your brand through your lens. A critical part of sharing your brand is by staying connected and building authentic relationships and getting your brand out there. Networking is how your brand will work for you and create opportunities in the future. You are who you connect with. Getting out and meeting new people outside of your comfort zone is how you can create new and exciting opportunities and share your personal brand.

Networking hacks!

(mostly for introverts)

  • Have a blog (or a microblog!)
  • Become a connector!
  • Speak at an event!
  • Organize a comparatively low effort event!
  • Talk to n people before leaving.
I am a huge introvert and I kind of hate a big thing about building your personal brand is networking. This is so far out of my comfort zone that sometimes it makes me really upset. However over the years I have discovered these hacks that I hope can help you too! The advantange of having a blog or of being on a microblogging site is you can take your time, formulate your thoughts, and engage in real dialogue with others. Become a connector, one person at a time. Each week or every two weeks ask a different person at work for coffee or lunch. you can make yourself professionally indispensable if you develop connections that enable you to break through silos, and identify and surmount knowledge gaps. Speaking is great, because you already have spent a lot of time thinking about whatever you're going to talk about and once you're done with the talk, people can come up to you and you already have something to talk about!

Pitfalls!

One of pitfalls that you might fall into as a junior engineer is glue work. Tanya Reily has an incredible talk about glue work, it's when the non-technical aspects of your job like writing the docs, establishing coding standards, setting up collaboration with other teams etc fall upon your shoulder because it's something that you really care about and thus a part of your personal brand. Brag documents help you track this work! Don't pigeonhole yourself!

What we owe to one another once we are "successful"?

  • Mentor folks!
  • Hype others around you!
  • Run brag doc workshops, setup networking events!
Once you can know and identify how to make your work visible, help other folks around you. Pay it forward! A lot of times the competition that we see if artifical competition. there is much more opportunity outside career ladders than within them, and by including those opportunities you'll make and feel more progress. Better yet, you'll find far more opportunities to partner with your peers, no longer competing for limited promotion slots.
